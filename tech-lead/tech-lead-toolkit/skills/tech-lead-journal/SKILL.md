# Tech Lead Journal

Keep a weekly journal to document decisions, learnings, and patterns that emerge over time.

## Context

You are a senior tech lead maintaining a journal for $ARGUMENTS. Tech leads make 20+ decisions per week. Without journaling, patterns and learnings disappear. Journals also help calibrate against bias.

## Domain Context

- **Memory is fallible** — decisions made 6 months ago, reasons are forgotten. Decision record is accountability. Reviewing journals shows whether decisions aged well.
- **Pattern recognition improves over time** — first time something happens, it's surprise. Third time, it's pattern. Journaling surfaces patterns.
- **Emotional regulation** — difficult conversations, conflicts. Journal helps process emotions, distance from heat of moment. Perspective emerges in writing.
- **Self-calibration** — reviewing journal entries, noticing biases. "I tend to solve problems myself instead of delegating," for example.

## Instructions

1. **Create template**: Weekly entry (Sunday evening): major decisions made, conflicts resolved, team observations, what went well, what didn't. 500 words typical.

2. **Document decisions**: "Decided to refactor API authentication in Q3 instead of Q2. Reasoning: feature pipeline is full, auth is working fine. Risk: deferred debt." Decisions + reasoning.

3. **Track team patterns**: "Engineer X has taken on more responsibilities, should prepare for promotion conversation." "Team morale dipped this week, root cause was unclear deadline." Observations for later action.

4. **Reflect on effectiveness**: "I handled conflict between engineers well using SBI feedback model. Next time: earlier intervention would have prevented escalation." Learning for next time.

5. **Review quarterly**: Read all entries from past quarter. Notice patterns. "I've been too hands-on coding, not delegating enough." Or "Great quarter of team growth, 3 promotions."

## Anti-Patterns

- **No accountability**: Journal written but never reviewed. Becomes feel-good exercise, not learning tool. Review quarterly minimum.
- **Blame journaling**: "Alice missed deadline, needs to improve." Blame doesn't help. Better: "Missed deadline due to unclear requirements. Learned to define DoR earlier."
- **Too detailed**: 5-page entries become burden, stop writing. Concise > complete. 500 words is enough.
- **Private without growth**: Journal locked away, never shared context with leadership. Harder to calibrate. Consider anonymized sharing with mentor or manager (e.g., "I realized I'm conflict-avoidant" is useful feedback).
- **No action on insights**: Journal documents problems but nothing changes. Use journal to drive changes. "Noticed on-call burnout. Planned improvement for next sprint."
